Get the healthy habit

"I always leave feeling happy!"

The 40 Foot, in Sandycove, Dublin, Ireland, has been a mecca for sea swimmers for over 250 years. We'd like to share with you why thousands of people enjoy a dip here every year and invite you to become one of "The Regulars"

Feel Strong

Young and old, athlete or not, the 40 Foot is a great place to take your daily exercise. Swimming is a low impact exercise, easy on the joints. Exercise aside, jumping in for even a couple of minutes provides a number of physical benefits. Exposure the cold water can increase metabolism and reduce inflamation and muscle pain

Clear the Head

There is something almost indescribable about plunging into the salty cold water. A release of dopamine or a surge of adrenalin, it does seem to matter what mood you are in when you arrive, you will leave with a smile on your face. Higher energy levels and energised to launch into the day are just some of the claims of the regular dippers.

A Sense of Community

This little haven is the ideal place to connect with friends that otherwise we struggle to find time to meet. Get some quality family time sharing the euphoria of a weekend dip and a hot chocolate afterwards. Drop down for a dip with the team after the match. Even if you turn up on your own it won't be long before you connect with the other regulars through this shared experience.

Connect with Nature

The smell of the sea and the noise of the waves is part what sets a dip in the 40 Foot apart from the local swimming pool. There is also the thrill of spotting one of the local seals. The seals tend to keep to themselves but they are always nearby, sometime basking on a rock or passing by to do fish for their breakfast. It feels like such a privalage to be sharing this beautful spot with them.

History of the 40 Foot

Origin of the name

The name "Forty Foot" is somewhat obscure. On an 1833 map, the Marine Road (located 1.5 km (0.93 mi) to the west) was named the Forty Foot Road, possibly because it was 40 ft (12 m) wide; the name may have been transferred to the swimming place, which was called the Forty-Foot Hole in the 19th century.

Other accounts claim the name was given by fishermen because it was forty feet (6+2‚ÅÑ3 fathoms) deep, but the water in the area is no deeper than 20 ft (6.1 m; 3.3 fathoms).[6] Others have attempted to link it to the 40th (the 2nd Somersetshire) Regiment of Foot, who supposedly bathed there, but they were stationed at Richmond Barracks in Inchicore

Sandycove Bathers Association

At first, it was exclusively a male bathing place, and Sandycove Bathers Association, a men's swimming club was established. Owing to its relative isolation and gender-restrictions it became a popular spot for nudists. The association resisted the efforts of women to use the 40 foot for many years.

Equal Rights for Women

On 24 July 1974, about a dozen of female equal-rights activists ("Dublin City Women's Invasion Force") went swimming, and sat with placards over the 'Gentelemen only' rules at the 40 Foot, starting a new era of mixed swimming.

In 1989, a group of women swam nude in protest against male nude swimming, watched by over 1000 onlookers. In 2014, the Sandycove Bathers Association finally ended the ban on women club members, and they may now use the onsite changing rooms and clubhouse kitchen.

Written About

James Joyce and Oliver St. John Gogarty once resided at the Martello tower together. It is now the James Joyce Tower and Museum. The opening section of Joyce's Ulysses is set here, with the characters Stephen Dedalus and Buck Mulligan being partly based on Joyce himself and Gogarty, respectively. Buck Mulligan described the sea as "The snotgreen sea. The scrotumtightening sea."

The Forty Foot also featured in the novels At Swim-Two-Birds by Flann O'Brien (1939), At Swim, Two Boys by Jamie O'Neill (2001) and Nessuna notizia dello scrittore scomparso by Daniele Bresciani (2017).
